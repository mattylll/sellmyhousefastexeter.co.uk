That buyer's purchaser may also need to sell, and so on. In Exeter, chains of four or five linked transactions are common, and longer chains are not unusual. Having your legal paperwork prepared in advance, including title deeds, property information forms, and fixtures and fittings lists, means your solicitor can respond quickly once a sale is agreed.</p><h3>Maintain Communication</h3><p>Chains collapse partly because of poor communication. Stay in regular contact with your estate agent and solicitor, and encourage them to maintain dialogue with the other parties in the chain. Proactive communication can identify emerging problems before they become terminal, and often a simple phone call can resolve a concern that might otherwise spiral into a withdrawal.</p><h3>Be Flexible on Timing</h3><p>Rigid deadlines can break chains. If a buyer further up the chain needs an extra week to complete their sale, accommodating that flexibility can save the entire transaction. Obviously, flexibility has limits, particularly if you have your own onward commitments, but where possible, a willingness to adjust dates can keep a chain together.</p><h2>What to Do When a Chain Breaks</h2><p>Despite your best efforts, chains can still collapse. If it happens, the immediate question is whether to relist through your estate agent and try again, or to consider an alternative route.</p><p>Relisting means going through the entire process again: marketing, viewings, offers, negotiations, and the formation of a new chain that carries the same risks as the old one.

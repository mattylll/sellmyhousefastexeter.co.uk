11:T1f6f,<h2>Why Exeter Landlords Are Selling</h2><p>The buy-to-let landscape in Exeter has shifted dramatically over the past decade. What was once a relatively straightforward investment, particularly in a city with a major university and strong rental demand, has become increasingly complex and less profitable for many smaller landlords. The combination of regulatory changes, tax reforms, and rising costs has prompted a growing number of Exeter landlords to consider their exit strategy.</p><p>Exeter's rental market has historically been robust. The University of Exeter draws over 20,000 students, many seeking private rented accommodation in areas like St Thomas, Pennsylvania, and the streets surrounding the Streatham Campus. The Royal Devon and Exeter Hospital, the Met Office, and the broader Devon economy generate demand from professionals and key workers. This rental demand pushed many Exeter residents and external investors into buy-to-let from the early 2000s onwards.</p><p>But the economics have changed. The phased removal of mortgage interest relief, the 3% stamp duty surcharge on additional properties, and tighter lending criteria for buy-to-let mortgages have all reduced returns. Meanwhile, the regulatory burden has increased: Energy Performance Certificate requirements, electrical safety regulations, the Tenant Fees Act, deposit protection rules, and the prospect of further reform under the Renters' Reform Bill have added administrative cost and compliance risk.</p><p>For many Exeter landlords with one or two properties, the calculation no longer works. The rental income, after tax, mortgage interest, management costs, maintenance, insurance, and compliance expenses, leaves a margin that does not justify the capital tied up and the risk involved. Selling up and redeploying the capital elsewhere has become the rational choice.</p><h2>The Challenges of Selling a Rental Property in Exeter</h2><h3>Tenanted Properties</h3><p>One of the biggest practical challenges for exiting landlords is the presence of tenants. Selling a tenanted property through traditional channels dramatically narrows the buyer pool. Owner-occupier buyers, who represent the majority of the market, generally want vacant possession. This means either selling to another investor at a discount, or serving notice and waiting for the tenants to leave.</p><p>Serving notice under Section 21 (where still applicable) or Section 8 requires careful compliance with legal requirements. The notice period, which may be two months or more, extends the timeline. If tenants do not leave voluntarily, the court process adds further months and cost. During this period, the property may deteriorate if the tenant-landlord relationship has broken down.</p><h3>Property Condition</h3><p>Rental properties in Exeter often show the wear that comes with tenanted use. Carpets, decorating, kitchens, and bathrooms may be functional but tired. Gardens may be neglected. The property may not present well for viewings, particularly compared to owner-occupied homes in the same area. This condition gap can suppress the achievable price on the open market.</p><h3>HMO Complications</h3><p>Landlords operating Houses in Multiple Occupation near the university face additional complexity. HMO licensing requirements, room size regulations, fire safety standards, and the specific planning considerations that apply to some HMOs all affect saleability. Converting an HMO back to a single-family dwelling may be necessary to attract owner-occupier buyers, but this involves cost and planning considerations.</p><h3>Tax on the Sale</h3><p>Capital Gains Tax applies to the profit on the sale of a buy-to-let property. The gain is calculated as the difference between the purchase price (plus allowable costs) and the sale price. For landlords who bought in Exeter ten or fifteen years ago, when prices were significantly lower, the gain can be substantial. CGT rates on property are currently 18% for basic rate taxpayers and 24% for higher rate taxpayers, with an annual exempt amount that partially offsets the liability.</p><h2>Your Options for Selling Rental Property in Exeter</h2><h3>Sell with Vacant Possession via Estate Agent</h3><p>This route achieves the highest gross price but requires the tenants to leave first. After serving notice and waiting for vacant possession, the property may need refurbishment before marketing. The timeline from deciding to sell to receiving funds can easily stretch to nine months or more. Estate agent fees of 1-2% plus VAT apply, along with legal costs and holding costs during the marketing period.</p><h3>Sell Tenanted via Estate Agent</h3><p>Marketing a tenanted property through an Exeter agent limits the buyer pool to investors. Investor buyers expect a discount reflecting the reduced control and the condition issues that tenanted properties often present. Expect a price 10-15% below vacant-possession value, a longer marketing period, and the same agent fees and legal costs.</p><h3>Sell to a Cash Buyer</h3><p>A cash buyer like us can purchase your Exeter rental property with tenants in place, in any condition, and complete in as little as two weeks. There are no agent fees, we cover basic legal costs, and you choose the completion date. The offer will be below full market value, but when compared against the alternative of serving notice, waiting months for vacant possession, refurbishing, paying agent fees, and risking a collapsed sale, the net outcome can be comparable, and the timeline is dramatically shorter.</p><h2>Specific Considerations for Exeter Landlords</h2><h3>University Area Properties</h3><p>Properties in the streets around the university, in St Thomas, and in Pennsylvania that have been let to students may carry HMO licences and be configured for multiple lets. If selling to an investor, the HMO status may add value. If selling for owner-occupation, reconfiguration costs need to be factored in. We buy both HMO and standard rental properties across Exeter.</p><h3>New-Build Rentals in Pinhoe</h3><p>Landlords who bought new-build properties in Pinhoe's expanding estates as buy-to-let investments face particular competition when selling. New phases of development from the original housebuilder offer buyers a brand-new alternative, often with incentives. Pricing competitively is essential, and a cash sale avoids the uncertainty of competing with developer marketing budgets.</p><h3>Period Properties</h3><p>Victorian and Edwardian rental properties in Heavitree and St Thomas may have deferred maintenance that a cash buyer can absorb but that would deter mortgage-dependent purchasers. If you have been managing maintenance to a rental standard rather than an owner-occupier standard, a cash buyer who purchases in any condition is likely to provide a more practical exit route.</p><h2>Tax Planning for Your Exit</h2><p>Before selling, consult a tax adviser about your CGT position. There are legitimate strategies to manage the liability, including timing the sale to use your annual exempt amount, offsetting allowable costs (purchase costs, improvement expenditure, legal fees), and coordinating with your spouse or civil partner to make use of their exempt amount.</p><p>If you own multiple Exeter rental properties, the sequence in which you sell can affect the overall tax position. Selling lower-gain properties first, or spreading sales across tax years, may reduce the total CGT payable. Professional advice is essential to optimise your position.</p><h2>Making a Clean Exit</h2><p>Exiting buy-to-let in Exeter does not have to be a protracted or stressful process. Whether you own a single terraced house in St Thomas or a portfolio of properties across the city, understanding your options and their financial implications allows you to make a clean break on your terms. For many landlords, the speed and certainty of a cash buyer provides the most practical route to redeploying their capital and moving on from the responsibilities of property

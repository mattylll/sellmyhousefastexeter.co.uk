Exeter Property Market Update: Trends, Prices, and What Sellers Need to Know

By Sell My House Fast Exeter

Exeter's Property Market in Context

Exeter's property market occupies a distinctive position within the South West and the wider UK housing landscape. As Devon's capital and largest city, it functions as the region's commercial, administrative, and cultural hub, drawing demand from a broad base of buyers including university staff, NHS professionals, families relocating from larger cities, and retirees seeking the Devon lifestyle with urban conveniences close at hand.

Average house prices in Exeter currently sit around £310,000, placing the city above the national average but below the premium levels seen in cities like Bath, Bristol, or Cambridge. This mid-range positioning creates a market that is accessible to a wide demographic, from first-time buyers stretching into Victorian terraces in St Thomas to affluent purchasers seeking period homes in St Leonard's or waterside living in Topsham.

The market's foundations are strong. Exeter's economy is diversified across education, healthcare, retail, and the growing digital and creative sectors. The Met Office's headquarters at Exeter Science Park brings highly skilled employment, while the Royal Devon and Exeter Hospital and the University of Exeter are among the county's largest employers. This employment base provides the demand fundamentals that support property values through economic cycles.

Price Trends Across Exeter's Neighbourhoods

Exeter's property market is not monolithic. Prices and trends vary significantly between neighbourhoods, and understanding these local dynamics is essential for sellers making informed decisions.

St Thomas has seen steady growth driven by its affordability relative to central Exeter, its transport links via St Thomas station, and the ongoing gentrification of Cowick Street. Victorian terraces that sold for £180,000 a decade ago now command £250,000 to £300,000, with the best examples exceeding that. The area's appeal to first-time buyers and young families sustains demand, though the condition of the older housing stock means that move-in-ready properties command a significant premium over those needing work.

Heavitree presents a broad price range reflecting its varied housing stock. The large Victorian and Edwardian houses along the main roads can achieve £400,000 to £600,000, while smaller terraces and ex-council properties provide entry points well below £250,000. The area's family-friendly character, good schools, and established community identity support consistent demand across all price brackets.

Pinhoe has experienced the most dramatic change. The expansion of new-build estates has transformed the area from a village of a few hundred homes to one of Exeter's most populated neighbourhoods. New-build prices typically range from £250,000 for smaller properties to over £400,000 for larger detached houses. The challenge for resellers is competition from ongoing new development, which can cap achievable prices for existing homes.

Topsham operates at a premium thanks to its estuary setting, period architecture, and village character. Average prices here sit well above the Exeter norm, with waterside properties and larger period houses regularly exceeding £500,000. The market is characterised by lower volume and longer marketing periods, reflecting the smaller buyer pool at this price level.

Alphington and Wonford provide more affordable options within Exeter's boundaries. Prices in both areas tend to sit below the city average, making them accessible to first-time buyers and investors. Demand is steady rather than spectacular, and properties needing significant updating can take longer to sell through traditional channels.

St Leonard's commands a premium reflecting its period housing stock, leafy character, and proximity to the university. Well-presented Victorian and Edwardian houses regularly achieve prices above £400,000, making it one of Exeter's most expensive residential areas outside Topsham.

Demand Drivers and Challenges

Several factors are shaping current demand in the Exeter market. The ongoing growth of the university, both in student numbers and staff, sustains a reliable demand base for both purchase and rental properties. The NHS's presence through the Royal Devon and Exeter Hospital generates a steady flow of healthcare professionals seeking housing, many on relatively short timescales that favour certainty over maximum price.

Remote and hybrid working patterns, accelerated by the pandemic, have boosted demand from buyers relocating from London and other major cities. Exeter's combination of urban amenities, proximity to Dartmoor and the coast, and good rail links to London make it an attractive option for those no longer tied to a daily commute. This has pushed prices upward, particularly in the more desirable neighbourhoods.

On the challenge side, mortgage interest rates remain above the ultra-low levels of recent years, affecting affordability for first-time buyers and limiting the amount that existing homeowners can borrow for their next purchase. This has cooled the market at the lower and middle price ranges, leading to longer marketing periods and more frequent price reductions.

The supply of new housing, particularly in Pinhoe and the emerging Cranbrook development east of the city, is adding to the available stock. While this helps address the overall housing shortage, it creates direct competition for resellers in affected areas and can suppress price growth for existing properties.

What This Means for Exeter Sellers

For Exeter homeowners considering a sale, the current market presents a mixed picture. Demand remains fundamentally strong thanks to the city's employment base, university, and lifestyle appeal. Well-presented properties in popular areas like St Leonard's, Heavitree, and Topsham continue to attract buyers and achieve good prices.

However, the days of properties selling within days of listing at asking price or above are largely behind us. Sellers need to price realistically, present their homes well, and be prepared for a marketing period that could stretch to several months. Properties needing work face a tougher market, as buyers factor renovation costs against their stretched budgets.

For sellers who need speed and certainty, the current market dynamics make a strong case for considering a cash buyer. The combination of longer average marketing times, higher fall-through rates driven by mortgage affordability concerns, and competition from new-build stock means that the traditional route carries more risk and takes longer than it did a few years ago.

Our cash buying service offers an alternative that is particularly relevant in today's market. A guaranteed sale at a known price, with completion on your timeline and no exposure to the uncertainties of the open market, provides a level of control that many Exeter homeowners find valuable, whether they are in Topsham's premium market or Wonford's more affordable segment.

Looking Ahead

Exeter's long-term property market outlook remains positive. The city's economic fundamentals are sound, the population is growing, and the quality of life on offer continues to attract new residents from across the country. Infrastructure improvements, including potential enhancements to the rail service and the ongoing development of Exeter Science Park, should support further growth.

The short to medium term is likely to see a more balanced market, with price growth moderating and sellers needing to work harder to achieve their asking prices. For those with time and a property that shows well, the traditional route will continue to deliver results. For those who need certainty, speed, or who face complications that make traditional sales difficult, the cash buyer route offers a reliable and proven alternative.
